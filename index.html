ZIP Code

Banks, insurers, advertisers and even hospitals routinely feed your ZIP code into models that guess what you can afford, how risky you are, and what you might buy. Five digits become a prediction about you. Some people think that is clever and fair. Others think it is a polite form of discrimination.

Below are four honest voices, two on each side. Tap a card to hear the rest of each argument. We do not take a side. You decide.

The case for ZIP code predictions

  • Geography really does carry signal. Flood risk, theft rates and repair costs differ street by street. Ignoring that just makes products more expensive for everyone.
  • It is not about who you are. The model never sees your race or your name, only an area average. That is far less personal than reading your private messages.
  • It keeps prices fair to the careful. Without local data, low risk people quietly subsidise high risk ones. Pricing by place rewards reality.
  • It can direct help, not just costs. The same maps that price insurance also flag neighbourhoods that need clinics, transit or disaster aid first.

Bottom line: data about places is honest, useful and already everywhere. Banning it does not make the risk disappear.

The case for using area data in public services

  • Limited budgets have to land somewhere. Area level need tells me where a mobile clinic or a cooling centre will save the most lives this summer.
  • It catches problems people do not report. Neighbourhood data flags lead pipes or asthma clusters long before individual complaints arrive.
  • It is faster than knocking on every door. In a flood or a heatwave, waiting for perfect individual data costs hours we do not have.
  • Used openly, it can shrink gaps. The same numbers that some people fear can be pointed at fixing the very inequality critics worry about.

Bottom line: refusing to look at where need is concentrated does not make the need fairer, it just makes it slower to reach.

The case against ZIP code predictions

  • It is a proxy for things we banned. In many places ZIP codes track race and wealth almost perfectly. Using them can recreate redlining while staying technically neutral.
  • You are punished for your neighbours. A careful person on a struggling street pays more for the behaviour of strangers they never met.
  • It locks inequality in place. Higher prices and worse offers make a struggling area struggle more. The prediction becomes a self fulfilling prophecy.
  • You cannot argue with it. The model is invisible and you never agreed to it. There is no desk to appeal to when an average overrides your real record.

Bottom line: a fact about a place becomes a verdict about a person, and the people it hurts rarely chose where they could afford to live.

The case against geography as a shortcut

  • Neutral inputs can hide banned ones. A postcode can carry race and religion so closely that the model discriminates without ever naming it.
  • Harm without intent is still harm. A company can mean well and still deny a generation of one neighbourhood the loans that build wealth.
  • It is almost impossible to challenge. People cannot see the model, cannot see the data and rarely learn that geography is why they were refused.
  • It freezes history in code. Maps drawn by past prejudice get fed into tomorrow's decisions, passing old unfairness forward as maths.

Bottom line: if an input reaches the same result as a banned one, calling it neutral does not make it fair.

The same five digits, four real places it shows up

Each of these is happening now. For every one, there is a genuine upside and a genuine harm. We lay them side by side so you can weigh them yourself.

Insurance priced by area

Car and home cover is often quoted from your postcode before anything else is known about you.

The upside

Local theft, flood and repair costs are real. Pricing them keeps cover affordable for careful, low risk areas.

The harm

A safe driver on a high claim street pays for strangers. The bill follows the postcode, not the person.

Mortgages and lending

Lenders have long used neighbourhood data to judge who gets a loan and at what rate.

The upside

Past repayment patterns in an area carry genuine information about risk that lenders price in.

The harm

This is the root of redlining. Whole neighbourhoods, mostly of one race, were locked out of buying homes for decades.

Online offers and ads

Shops and platforms tune prices, deals and stock to the area you are browsing from.

The upside

You see local stock, nearby pickup and offers that actually apply where you live.

The harm

Poorer postcodes can be quietly shown worse prices and fewer good offers, paying more for the same thing.

Emergency and public health

Cities use area data to decide where clinics, aid and warnings go first.

The upside

Help reaches the streets that need it fastest in a flood, a heatwave or an outbreak.

The harm

A risky area label can stick. It can stigmatise residents and shape how outsiders treat a whole community.
