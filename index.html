ZIP Code

Banks, insurers, advertisers and even hospitals routinely feed your ZIP code into models that guess what you can afford, how risky you are, and what you might buy. Five digits become a prediction about you. Some people think that is clever and fair. Others think it is a polite form of discrimination.

The case for ZIP-code predictions

  • Geography really does carry signal. Flood risk, theft rates and repair costs differ street by street. Ignoring that just makes products more expensive for everyone.
  • It's not about who you are. The model never sees your race or your name — only an area average. That's far less personal than reading your private messages.
  • It keeps prices fair to the careful. Without local data, low-risk people quietly subsidise high-risk ones. Pricing by place rewards reality.
  • It can direct help, not just costs. The same maps that price insurance also flag neighbourhoods that need clinics, transit, or disaster aid first.

Bottom line: data about places is honest, useful, and already everywhere — banning it doesn't make the risk disappear.

The case against ZIP-code predictions

  • It's a proxy for things we banned. In many places ZIP codes track race and wealth almost perfectly. Using them can recreate redlining while staying technically “neutral.”
  • You're punished for your neighbours. A careful person on a “bad” street pays more for the behaviour of strangers they never met.
  • It locks inequality in place. Higher prices and worse offers make a struggling area struggle more — the prediction becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.
  • You can't argue with it. The model is invisible and you never consented. There's no desk to appeal to when an average overrides your real record.

Bottom line: a fact about a place becomes a verdict about a person — and the people it hurts rarely chose where they could afford to live.
